# Where to Get Your Ice Skates Sharpened in Nashville

If you skate in Nashville for any length of time, you will eventually need your blades sharpened, and you will quickly find there is no single, clear answer online for where to go. The options are scattered across rink pro shops, a dedicated skate shop, and a couple of independent sharpeners working out of the rinks. This guide gathers them in one place for both figure skaters and hockey players, including one important recent change: the pro shop at Centennial Sportsplex is no longer the easy answer it used to be.

A good sharpening makes more difference than most new skaters expect. Dull blades slide out from under you and make stopping and turning feel impossible, while a fresh, properly done edge grips the ice and makes everything from a first glide to a crossover feel more secure. A bad or wrong sharpening, on the other hand, can ruin a session, which is exactly why where you take your skates matters.

## Where can you get ice skates sharpened in Nashville?

In the Nashville area, your best options are Rainbo Sports and Skating (a dedicated skate shop and the go-to for figure skaters), the Ford Ice Center pro shops, and an independent sharpener called Yuri's that works out of the Gary Force Acura Ice Arena in Nolensville. Hockey players also have a couple of sporting-goods options. Note that the on-site pro shop at Centennial Sportsplex has closed under the rink's new management, so it is no longer a reliable walk-in choice. Here is the full rundown.

Rainbo Sports and Skating is the dedicated skate shop in the Nashville area and the name local coaches and figure skaters mention first. They handle sharpening for competitive figure skaters, recreational skaters, coaches, and hockey players, along with blade work, mounting, and repairs. Because skate sharpening is what a specialist shop does all day, this is the safe choice when you care about a consistent, correct edge, which figure skaters especially do. For figure skaters, a dedicated shop matters more than it might sound. Figure blades have a different profile than hockey blades, a more delicate edge, and a toe pick that a careless grind can damage, so handing them to someone who sharpens figure skates every day is worth the drive.

## Figure skates versus hockey skates: why the difference matters

It is worth understanding what sharpening actually does, because it changes who you should trust with your skates. A sharpening grinds a hollow groove down the length of the blade, leaving two edges, an inside edge and an outside edge, that bite into the ice. How deep that hollow is, often called the radius of hollow, changes how much the blade grips versus glides. A deeper hollow grips harder and is common for figure skaters and lighter skaters; a shallower hollow glides more and suits many hockey players and bigger skaters.

Figure blades add a wrinkle: the toe pick at the front and a curved rocker that a sharpener has to respect. A shop that sharpens figure skates regularly knows to protect the toe pick and keep the rocker consistent. A generic grind can dull the pick or flatten the curve, which a figure skater will feel immediately in their jumps and spins. This is the single biggest reason to take figure skates to a specialist like Rainbo rather than the nearest counter.

## How often should you sharpen, and what to ask for

For a recreational skater on public sessions, sharpening once a season or whenever the blades start sliding and feeling dull is usually plenty. Skaters who train several times a week, work on jumps, or play hockey will go more often, sometimes every few weeks, because they wear and nick the edges faster. The honest test is feel: when stopping and turning take more effort than they used to, or the blade chatters instead of biting, it is time.

When you drop your skates off, a few details help the sharpener get it right. Tell them whether the skates are figure or hockey, mention your level and weight if they ask (it informs the hollow they choose), and if you have had a hollow you liked before, ask them to match it. If you are brand new and have no idea, a good shop will pick a sensible standard hollow for your skate type, and you can adjust from there once you know what you like. Allow a day for turnaround unless the shop tells you otherwise, and never assume same-day service at a busy rink pro shop.

## For hard-to-find or specialty sharpening, ask a coach

Nashville is a smaller skating town than a Boston or a Minneapolis, and some of the best sharpening happens by word of mouth, through coaches who know exactly who to send a skater to for a particular blade or a tricky repair. Those arrangements are private and not something to look up online. If you need specialty work and the shops above are not the right fit, the move is to ask a local coach, who can point you to the right person. ## Tips for keeping your edges between sharpenings

A few habits stretch the life of a good sharpening:

- Put on hard guards only when you are off the ice and walking on rubber mats, and never walk on concrete or hard floors in bare blades.
- Dry your blades completely with a soft cloth after every session, because moisture left on steel causes rust that ruins an edge.
- Use soft, absorbent soakers to store the skates between sessions, not the hard walking guards, which trap moisture.
- Watch for nicks. If you catch a screw, a door track, or a hard floor, the edge can develop a burr that no amount of skating will smooth out, and it is worth a touch-up.

Treat the blades well and a single good sharpening lasts a long time. Neglect them and even a perfect sharpening will not save you.
